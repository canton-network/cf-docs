The Global Synchronizer Foundation (GSF), now operating as the [Canton Foundation](https://canton.foundation), is a non-profit body under the Linux Foundation that governs and grows the Global Synchronizer. It provides organizational structure, processes, and oversight complementing the on-chain governance executed by Super Validators (SVs).

## Role of the Foundation

The foundation serves three functions:

- **Governance facilitation** -- It defines and maintains the rules and procedures under which the Global Synchronizer operates, from validator onboarding requirements to tokenomics parameters.
- **Ecosystem growth** -- It manages outreach, membership programs, and working groups that bring new participants into the network. Premier members include major financial institutions and technology companies.
- **Operational transparency** -- It publishes network status information, maintains public documentation across [DevNet](https://docs.dev.sync.global/), [TestNet](https://docs.test.sync.global/), and [MainNet](https://docs.sync.global/) environments, and hosts mailing lists for community discussion at [lists.sync.global](https://lists.sync.global).

The foundation also operates its own SV node and participates in governance votes on behalf of its members. That node carries the same voting weight as any other SV -- it has no special authority within the on-chain governance system.

## Policy Areas

GSF policies cover the following areas:

- **Validator onboarding** -- Requirements and procedures for new validators joining the Global Synchronizer. Each validator must be sponsored by an existing SV, which generates a one-time onboarding secret (valid for 48 hours). The foundation sets the eligibility criteria and operational expectations for new validators.
- **SV membership** -- Criteria for becoming a Super Validator, including identity verification, infrastructure requirements, and the governance vote needed for onboarding. Adding a new SV requires a supermajority vote. Offboarding an SV requires unanimous agreement from all current SVs.
- **Network parameters** -- Policies governing synchronizer configuration such as traffic pricing (`extraTrafficPrice`), read-vs-write scaling factors, round duration, and reconciliation intervals. These values directly affect the cost and performance of the network.
- **Tokenomics parameters** -- Policies around Canton Coin issuance curves, fee schedules, the CC-USD conversion rate, and reward weight distributions. The foundation's [tokenomics committee](https://lists.sync.global/g/tokenomics/topics) discusses proposed changes before they go to an SV vote.
- **Featured applications** -- The process for granting or revoking featured app status, which determines whether an application provider earns enhanced minting rewards. The foundation reviews submissions and tracks them through the tokenomics committee.
- **Software upgrades** -- Policies for coordinating Daml package upgrades and Splice releases across the SV operator group, including the governance votes required to activate new package configurations on the network.

## On-Chain Enforcement via the DSO Party

The technical backbone of GSF policy enforcement is the DSO party -- a decentralized Daml party hosted across all SV nodes. The DSO party operates with a Byzantine Fault Tolerant (BFT) confirmation threshold: actions require confirmation from `ceiling(numSVs * 2 / 3)` SVs. The system tolerates up to `f` faulty or dishonest nodes, where `f = floor((n - 1) / 3)` and `n` is the total number of SVs.

Governance actions are executed through Daml contracts in the `splice-dso-governance` package, primarily through two contract types:

- **`DsoRules`** -- Governs SV onboarding and offboarding, reward weights, featured app rights, and network-level configuration parameters stored in `DsoRulesConfig`.
- **`AmuletRules`** -- Governs Canton Coin economics: fee schedules, issuance curves, traffic pricing, and the CC-USD conversion rate.

When SVs vote on a parameter change or membership decision, the vote is recorded on-chain as a `VoteRequest` contract. Each request specifies the proposed action, a `voteBefore` deadline, and an optional `targetEffectiveAt` timestamp for scheduled changes. Once enough SVs accept the request (meeting the `requiredNumVotes` threshold), the action executes automatically.

Some governance values use a median-based approach instead of explicit voting. For the CC-USD conversion rate, each SV operator publishes their preferred value on-chain, and the DSO automatically selects the median. This produces stable, aggregate-preference pricing that reflects the positions of roughly two-thirds of SV operators, making it resistant to manipulation by any single SV.

The combination of on-chain voting and decentralized automation provides three guarantees for participants who trust that no more than `f` SVs are dishonest:

- **Valid transactions** -- Every DSO-confirmed transaction satisfies Daml's ledger validity model, so no invalid governance action can be pushed through.
- **Timely automation** -- Routine operational actions like mining round advancement and reward issuance execute without delay, because each SV node runs automation that creates confirmations as soon as preconditions are met.
- **Predictable parameters** -- Fees and configuration values reflect the aggregate preferences of at least two-thirds of SV operators, preventing any single actor from shifting parameters in their favor.

For full details on the voting mechanics and all governance action types, see the [SV Governance Reference](/docs-main/overview/reference/sv-governance-reference).

## Off-Chain Enforcement

Not everything can be encoded in Daml contracts. The foundation also enforces policies through its charter, membership agreements, and operational procedures. Off-chain governance covers:

- **Eligibility criteria** for new SVs and validators, including reputational and infrastructure requirements that go beyond what on-chain identity verification can capture.
- **Dispute resolution** between network participants.
- **Upgrade coordination** -- scheduling maintenance windows, communicating version requirements, and ensuring SVs upgrade in a synchronized manner.
- **CIP review** -- evaluating proposals through committee discussion before they reach the on-chain voting stage.

The foundation's mailing lists at [lists.sync.global](https://lists.sync.global) are where much of this off-chain discussion happens. The tokenomics committee list, in particular, is the venue for discussing parameter adjustments and featured app requests.

## Relationship to CIPs

Canton Improvement Proposals (CIPs) are the formal mechanism for proposing changes to Canton Network standards, protocols, and governance procedures. The [CIP repository](https://github.com/global-synchronizer-foundation/cips) hosts all proposals.

The relationship between CIPs and GSF policies works as follows:

1. A CIP proposes a change -- for example, [CIP-0042](https://github.com/global-synchronizer-foundation/cips/blob/main/cip-0042/cip-0042.md) specifying that a standard CC transfer should cost 1 USD, or [CIP-0066](https://github.com/global-synchronizer-foundation/cips/blob/main/cip-0066/cip-0066.md) defining how unclaimed SV rewards can be minted from the unclaimed pool.
2. The relevant GSF committee (such as the tokenomics committee) reviews and discusses the proposal.
3. If the CIP is accepted, the foundation enacts corresponding policy. For technical changes, this means SVs submit a governance vote to update the on-chain configuration. For process changes, the foundation updates its operational procedures.

Not all CIPs require on-chain votes. Informational and process CIPs may result in updated documentation or operational guidelines rather than contract parameter changes. See [CIPs Introduction](/docs-main/overview/understand/cips-introduction) for the full CIP lifecycle.

## SV Voting for Parameter Changes

Parameter changes follow a specific on-chain flow:

1. An SV creates a `VoteRequest` specifying the proposed configuration update (for example, a new `extraTrafficPrice` value on `AmuletRules`).
2. Other SVs review the proposal in the Governance tab of the SV web UI and cast accept or reject votes.
3. If `requiredNumVotes` SVs accept, the change takes effect -- either immediately or at a scheduled `targetEffectiveAt` time.
4. If `requiredNumVotes` SVs reject, the request is archived. If the `voteBefore` deadline passes without enough votes in either direction, the request expires.

SVs can change their votes on requests with a `targetEffectiveAt` date until the effective time arrives. This allows the group to reconsider scheduled changes as new information becomes available.

Changes to SV reward weights must also be reflected in the [GSF configs repository](https://github.com/global-synchronizer-foundation/configs) so the correct values persist across onboarding and reonboarding events. Traffic parameters require periodic recalibration as actual costs change with the number of SVs and the Canton protocol version.
